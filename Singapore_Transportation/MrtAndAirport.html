As part of the infrastructure visualization, a focus has been placed on Singapore's subway system. Initiated as part of the government's urban planning strategies and to address traffic management needs, the MRT system's first line, the North-South Line, was completed in 1987 (Newman and Kenworthy, 1999). Over the years, the system has undergone expansion, contributing significantly to Singapore's urban development and mobility (Barter, 2008).The role of Changi Airport, Singapore's primary civilian airport, is also emphasized. Since its inception in 1981, Changi has been one of the largest transportation hubs in Southeast Asia and has experienced continuous expansion to accommodate increasing air traffic and meet the diverse needs of international travelers (Phang and Foo, 2002).

It appears that the largest volume of exports and imports is transacted with other Asian countries, further illustrating the close trade ties Singapore shares with the region. The significant amount of imports from Mainland China and Europe, along with considerable exports to Oceania and Mainland China, reveal Singapore's diversified and global trade connections.

Singapore's trade strategy is notably anchored in its geographical position and economic requirements. As a nation limited by its size, it necessitates substantial imports of goods and resources from abroad. At the same time, Singapore's export focus on high-tech products and services echoes its economic growth strategy, which leverages trade interactions with different regions globally (Heng, 1997; Huff, 1994; Ho, 2003).
