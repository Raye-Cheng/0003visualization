Singapore has emerged as a leading example of a smart city, offering valuable lessons and best practices for other urban centers looking to embark on similar journeys.

City-states like Hong Kong and Singapore have emerged as significant players in the global economy due to their unique positions and dynamic industrial restructuring. Chiu (2018) and Abeysinghe and Choy (2009) have provided comprehensive analyses of Singapore's economic growth and development by examining various aspects, including GDP growth, underground development, and the evolution of Singapore's industries.

We will explore the critical connections between these three points and their overall impact on Singapore's economic trajectory.

Comparing with other Asian countries

Starting in 1961, the GDP per capita of Singapore and Hong Kong has always been very close, but much lower than that of Japan at the same time. By the mid-2000s, the GDP per capita of Singapore had overtaken Japan's and soon became the top one in Asia since 2009.
How this rapid economic development changed the city?

Singapore's impressive GDP growth has been driven by its openness to trade, investment in infrastructure, and human capital development (Abeysinghe & Choy, 2009).
The government's strategic policies, such as liberalization and financial sector development, have facilitated its integration into the global economy and enabled it to become a key economic hub in Asia (Chiu, 2018).

GPD Growth

Singapore's overall GDP growth has mostly been between 6% and 12%, but there have been five periods of negative growth and two periods of high growth.
The details are as follows:

1964:The economy was in decline.
1965–1985: Singapore became independent and entered 20 years of rapid development.
1985: The legacy of rapid growth was the worst economic crisis in two decades.
1985–1997: The economy began to recover.
1998: The Asian financial crisis slowed the economy down again.
2008: Affected by the world financial crisis and its development almost stopped.
2010: Singapore's economic development reached a historical peak.
2019: Affected by the global epidemic, its economic development hit a low point.
2019–now: The economy has recovered slowly.

Population change in different industries

There are four categories of industry in Singapore: Manufacturing, Construction, Service and Others. The proportion of service increased mostly during the past 23 years. The proportion of manufacturing decreased slightly, for the industry of construction and others, they almost kept stable.

Traffic influence and infrastructure

The city-state has invested significantly in underground infrastructure projects, such as the Mass Rapid Transit (MRT) system and subterranean spaces for commercial, retail, and residential purposes. These developments have not only increased land utilization efficiency but have also contributed to Singapore's economic growth and competitiveness (Chiu, 2018).
